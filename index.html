About me▶

My journey began in New Delhi. I wanted to be an athlete for the longest time, until a robotics class in seventh grade changed everything. My teacher's passion for the subject pulled me in, and robotics eventually gave way to computer science.

I studied at City University of Hong Kong on a full tuition scholarship — globally ranked #50 by QS at the time. Hong Kong was formative in more ways than one. I lived through the 2019 protests; one night, we filed out through a gap in the dorm wall hidden behind a cabinet, made our way past toppled buses, and reached a coach evacuating international students from the city. I saw smoke grenades, Molotov cocktails, tear gas, and broken infrastructure. A once-in-a-lifetime experience.

I interned at HSBC with their portfolio management team, learning how large institutions operate at scale. I then moved to New York for a master's at NYU on a partial tuition scholarship, where I took some of the most rigorous courses on offer and built my own search engine from scratch.

My first real tech experience was at Fileread, a legal-tech startup, where I interned twice before joining full-time. I built a document ingestion pipeline handling images, tables, and unstructured text, and shipped my first agent — a self-healing code agent. I found that programming languages have a sparser vocabulary than natural language, meaning keywords sit further apart in embedding space and hallucinations drop significantly. Out of curiosity, I introduced a bug into the agent's own codebase. The agent found it and fixed itself. That experiment convinced me that guardrails, not just capability, define a reliable agent.

I later joined an early-stage startup where I built several complex agents, deepened my understanding of AI-based retrieval, and learned what creative problem-solving looks like in genuine chaos. Working extensively with technical documents surfaced another insight: semantic search loses its edge when terminology is binary — a keyword is either in the document or it isn't, so embedding similarity adds little signal. The better approach was to hand the LLM the document's index and let it navigate to the right section itself, reading only what was relevant rather than retrieving by similarity.
